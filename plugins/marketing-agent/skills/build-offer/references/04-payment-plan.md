# The Pricing Hacks™ (Payment Plan)

## Position on the Seesaw
Payment Plan sits on the **RISK** side. It takes the money objection completely off the table — not by discounting, but by changing the payment terms.

## The Golden Rule
**Change the payment TERMS, not the payment AMOUNT.**

Don't discount. Don't undercharge. Don't over-deliver and burn out. Instead, make the same amount easier to pay.

## The Three Payment Tiers

### 1. Pay In Full (PIF) — Upfront
- Client saves money (typically 2 months' worth)
- You get a chunk of cash for growth (team, ads, reinvestment)
- Frame: "You can pay upfront and save some money"

### 2. Monthly Installments
- Easier on client's cash flow
- Recurring predictable revenue for you
- Installments should be HIGHER total than PIF (you're the bank, financing them)
- Frame: "You can pay in installments — easier on your cash flow"

### 3. Weekly Payments
- Smallest number feels most affordable ($600/week vs $2,400/month)
- You actually make MORE money: 52 weeks > 48 weeks (12 months × 4)
- You get 13 months of payments for 12 months of work
- Frame: "You can get started for just $X per week"

## The Weekly Payment Math

If your monthly price is $2,000/month × 12 months = $24,000 total
If weekly: $2,000 ÷ 4 = $500/week × 52 weeks = $26,000 total

You make $2,000 MORE and it feels cheaper to the client. Win-win.

## When NOT to Do Weekly

Two exceptions:
1. **Spending on ads and need 30-day payback** — If you need your CAC (client acquisition cost) back within 30 days, weekly spreads it too thin
2. **Cash flow is tight right now** — If you need the bulk of cash upfront, don't offer weekly

If neither applies, weekly is almost always the right move.

## The 25% Reactivation Play

Email everyone who said no this month: "Hey, I've been thinking about you and the goal we talked about. I've got an idea — we can get you started for $X per week."

**25% of people who already said no will say yes.** They found money without doing anything — you just changed the terms.

## Three Numbers to Know

1. **CAC** (Client Acquisition Cost) — What it costs to get a client
2. **LTV** (Lifetime Value) — What a client spends over their entire time with you
3. **30-Day Payback** — How much money comes back in the first 30 days

If CAC < LTV, you're winning. If 30-day payback > CAC, you can scale aggressively.

## Sales Call Language

> "So there's two ways you can pay. You can pay upfront and save some money, or you can go on a payment plan and it's easier on your cash flow. Both options are completely fine with me."

> "The program is $20K upfront, or 12 payments of $2,000 a month."

> Or with three options: "$20K upfront, $2,000/month, or $500/week."

Notice: upfront = discount framing. Installments = cash flow framing. Weekly = smallest number framing.

## The Sponsor-a-Child Principle

Those TV ads could have said "$30 a month." Instead they said "just $1 a day." Same money, completely different emotional response. Break the number down to the smallest believable unit.

A client charging $1,200/week — that's actually $62K/year. But people focus on the weekly number, not the total contract.

## Pricing Best Practices

| Principle | What It Means |
|-----------|---------------|
| ROI framing | "Give me $10, I'll give you $100 back" |
| Pay plan language | Use "upfront saves / installments easier on cash flow" |
| Align with outcomes | Price should feel proportional to the result |
| Use 3's and 5's | Round numbers in 3s and 5s feel natural ($1,500, $2,000, $2,500, $5,000) |

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Undercharging
Don't discount because people say no. The price isn't the problem — the TERMS or the OFFER is. Fix the offer, change the terms, but don't cut the price.

### Mistake 2: No Payment Options
Just saying "$20K, take it or leave it" means anyone without $20K liquid says no. Always offer at least upfront + installments.

### Mistake 3: Same Total for All Options
The installment total should be higher than PIF. You're financing them — that has value. PIF should genuinely save them money (typically ~2 months' worth).

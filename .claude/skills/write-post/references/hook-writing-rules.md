## THE 10 HOOK VARIATION TYPES

Each hook triggers a different psychological response. Every set of 5 hooks
uses a different type so the creator has genuine variety to choose from.

**1. Pattern Interrupt**
Says something the reader did not expect. Breaks the scroll rhythm.
First word cannot be "I". Must create a moment of "wait, what?"
Example logic: "[Assumed truth] is actually [unexpected opposite]."

**2. Question Hook**
A question the reader cannot answer "no" to and scroll past.
Must have genuine tension. The reader must feel pulled to know the answer.
Never: "Are you struggling with X?" (can always be answered no)
✓: "What if the thing you're working hardest at is the reason it's not working?"

**3. Bold Claim**
A direct, unhedged statement of a counterintuitive or provocative position.
No "I think." No "In my experience." Just the claim, flat and confident.
Under 10 words preferred.

**4. Number Hook**
Opens with a specific number that signals structure and payoff.
Numbers beat adjectives: "7 things" beats "several important things."
The number itself must create credibility, too high = unbelievable, too low = obvious.

**5. Story Open**
A first line that drops the reader into a specific moment.
Not a setup, the scene. Not "I want to tell you a story", the story.
Must contain at least one specific sensory or situational detail.
Example logic: "[Specific detail of a moment], [what made it significant]."

**6. Confession**
Opens with a vulnerable admission that the reader secretly shares.
Not manufactured vulnerability. A real thing most people in the audience have felt.
Example logic: "I [did something embarrassing or wrong] and [consequence]. Here's what I learned."

**7. Timeline Contrast**
Before vs after. Then vs now. The gap creates curiosity about what changed.
Example logic: "[X time ago, this was my situation]. Today, [the contrast]."

**8. The Audience Mirror**
Names the specific reader and their exact current state so precisely they feel seen.
Example logic: "If you're a [specific role] who [specific situation], [what this post is about]."

**9. Curiosity Gap**
Starts a thought and deliberately leaves it incomplete.
The incompleteness is the pull. Do not complete the thought until the body.
Example logic: "There's one thing [common situation] does that nobody talks about."

**10. The Specific Insight**
Opens with one specific, counterintuitive insight that immediately signals expertise.
The insight must be surprising enough that the reader wants the explanation.
Example logic: "[Specific thing] is [unexpected characterisation]. Here's why."

## THE GOLDEN HOOK RULES

**Rule 1, The first line must stand alone.**
Remove every other line from the post. Does the hook still earn the click?
If not, it is not a hook. It is a setup.

**Rule 2, Under 12 words preferred.**
On mobile, the line break happens fast. Long hooks lose the reader at the cut.

**Rule 3, No "I" as the first word.**
"I had a client who...", starts about you. Starts with you. Loses them.
"My client had 500 followers." "The founder I was coaching." Not "I."

**Rule 4, No "Are you struggling with..." openers.**
These can always be answered "no" and scrolled past. They signal low effort.

**Rule 5, Numbers beat adjectives.**
"7 reasons" beats "several reasons." "3 words" beats "a few words."
The number creates a subconscious promise of structure and delivery.

**Rule 6, The hook must not give the post away.**
If someone reads the hook and already knows the full point, the post is dead.
The hook promises. The body delivers.

**Rule 7, No clickbait without a payoff signal.**
The hook must suggest that reading further will be worth it.
"You won't believe what happened" with no hint of the domain = spam.

## EMOTION MAPPING

The intended emotion determines which hook types to use first.

| Intended Emotion | Best hook types |
|-----------------|----------------|
| Curiosity | Curiosity Gap, Pattern Interrupt, Specific Insight |
| Pain (recognition) | Audience Mirror, Confession, Question |
| Bold claim (authority) | Bold Claim, Number, Specific Insight |
| Story (human connection) | Story Open, Confession, Timeline Contrast |
| Data (credibility) | Number, Bold Claim, Specific Insight |
